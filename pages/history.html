30s-Legend

During this period, Bob Marley became a global icon of reggae and the Rastafarian movement. In 1976, he survived an assassination attempt at his home in Kingston, but this did not deter him in his music career. In 1977, The Wailers released the album "Exodus", featuring hits like "Jamming" and "Three Little Birds". Despite his rising fame, Marley battled cancer that had spread throughout his body. Nonetheless, he continued to perform and promote peace and unity through his music. Bob Marley passed away on May 11, 1981, at the age of 36, in Miami, Florida, due to complications related to cancer. His legacy endures as one of the most influential musicians of the 20th century.

20s-30s

During this decade, Bob Marley and The Wailers emerged as prominent figures in the Jamaican music scene. In 1972, they signed with Island Records and released their first international album, "Catch a Fire". Marley became an advocate for the Rastafarian movement, and his music began to reflect his spiritual and political beliefs. In 1974, The Wailers released the album "Natty Dread", which included hits like "No Woman, No Cry" and "Revolution".

10s-20s

In 1957, Bob Marley moved to Kingston, the capital of Jamaica, where he lived with his mother. During his teenage years, he became even more interested in music and formed his first band, The Wailers, with Peter Tosh and Bunny Wailer. At the age of 18, Marley married Rita Anderson and worked various jobs to support his family while continuing his music career.

Born-10s

Bob Marley was born on February 6, 1945, in Nine Mile, Jamaica. He spent his childhood in the rural community of Saint Ann, where he was raised by his mother, Cedella Booker, after his father, Norval Marley, left them. During this time, Marley showed early interest in music and began experimenting with the guitar.
